The Life of Jose Rizal

José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda (June 19, 1861 – December 30, 1896) was a Filipino nationalist, novelist, and key figure in the Philippine Revolution. He is considered the national hero of the Philippines.

Remarkable Fact

Rizal was a polymath who spoke 22 languages including Spanish, French, German, English, Italian, Japanese, Chinese, and various Philippine languages.

Born in Calamba, Laguna, Rizal was the seventh of eleven children of Francisco Rizal Mercado and Teodora Alonso Realonda. He attended the Ateneo Municipal de Manila and later studied medicine at the University of Santo Tomas in Manila. He continued his studies in Madrid, Spain, and traveled throughout Europe.

Rizal's two most famous novels, Noli Me Tangere (1887) and El Filibusterismo (1891), exposed the abuses of the Spanish colonial government and the Catholic Church in the Philippines. These works inspired the Philippine Revolution against Spanish rule.

After returning to the Philippines in 1892, Rizal founded the civic organization La Liga Filipina. He was soon exiled to Dapitan in Mindanao, where he practiced medicine, established a school, and engaged in various scientific and civic projects.

In 1896, Rizal was arrested, tried for sedition, and executed by firing squad in Manila at age 35. His martyrdom made him a symbol of the fight for Philippine independence.

Family Background

Rizal came from a wealthy and educated family. His father, Francisco Mercado, was a prosperous farmer who rented land from the Dominican order. His mother, Teodora Alonso, was highly educated and taught Rizal to read and write at an early age.

Family Name Change

The family name "Rizal" was adopted in 1849 when Governor-General Narciso Clavería ordered all Filipino families to choose new surnames from a list. The name "Rizal" was derived from the Spanish word "ricial," meaning "green field."

Education

Rizal's educational journey was remarkable:

  • Early education under his mother and private tutors
  • Ateneo Municipal de Manila (1872-1877) - earned Bachelor of Arts degree
  • University of Santo Tomas (1877-1882) - studied philosophy and medicine
  • Universidad Central de Madrid (1882-1885) - earned licentiate in medicine and philosophy
  • Studied ophthalmology in Paris and Heidelberg

Noli Me Tangere

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

Original Title: Noli Me Tángere (Latin for "Touch Me Not")

Language: Spanish

Genre: Novel, Satire, Political fiction

Synopsis

The novel tells the story of Juan Crisóstomo Ibarra, a young Filipino who returns to the Philippines after seven years of study in Europe. Through Ibarra's experiences, Rizal exposes the abuses of the Spanish colonial government and the Catholic Church in the Philippines.

The novel's characters represent various sectors of Philippine society under Spanish rule, from the abusive friars to the oppressed natives. The title "Noli Me Tangere" alludes to a biblical phrase that Rizal uses to symbolize the cancer of colonial oppression that afflicts the country.

Key Themes

  • Colonial abuse and corruption
  • Social injustice
  • Nationalism and reform
  • Religion and hypocrisy
  • Education as liberation

Historical Impact

The novel played a crucial role in awakening Filipino nationalism and inspiring the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. It was banned in the Philippines, and possession of a copy could lead to imprisonment or exile.

El Filibusterismo

El Filibusterismo (1891)

Original Title: El Filibusterismo (The Reign of Greed)

Language: Spanish

Genre: Novel, Political fiction

Synopsis

This sequel to Noli Me Tangere follows the return of Simoun (the transformed Ibarra) to the Philippines as a wealthy jeweler. Disillusioned by the failure of his reformist efforts, Simoun now seeks violent revolution to overthrow the Spanish regime.

The novel presents a darker, more radical perspective than its predecessor, exploring the consequences when peaceful reform fails. The title refers to "filibustero" - a term used by Spanish authorities to describe revolutionaries.

Key Themes

  • Revolution vs. reform
  • Colonial oppression
  • Corruption in the colonial system
  • The failure of assimilation
  • The role of violence in social change

Historical Impact

While less popular than Noli Me Tangere, El Filibusterismo had a profound impact on revolutionary thinkers. It presents a more radical vision of change that would influence later revolutionary movements.

Mi Último Adiós

Mi Último Adiós (1896)

English Title: My Last Farewell

Language: Spanish

Genre: Poem

Synopsis

This 14-verse poem was written by Rizal on the eve of his execution. It expresses his love for his country and his willingness to die for it. The poem was hidden in an alcohol lamp and given to his family after his death.

The poem does not explicitly mention the Philippines, but its references to the "Pearl of the Orient Seas" and other imagery clearly point to his homeland. It combines themes of patriotism, sacrifice, and hope for the future.

Key Themes

  • Patriotism and love of country
  • Sacrifice for a greater cause
  • Hope for the nation's future
  • Death and immortality
  • The beauty of the Philippines

Historical Impact

The poem became a rallying cry for Filipino revolutionaries and remains one of the most famous literary works in Philippine history. It is memorized by schoolchildren and recited at patriotic events.

The Indolence of the Filipinos

The Indolence of the Filipinos (1890)

Original Title: Sobre la indolencia de los filipinos

Language: Spanish

Genre: Essay

Synopsis

This essay was written as a response to Spanish accusations that Filipinos were inherently lazy. Rizal argues that what Spaniards perceive as indolence is actually a rational response to colonial oppression and the destruction of traditional economic systems.

Rizal examines historical and social factors that have contributed to the economic stagnation of the Philippines under Spanish rule, including: the destruction of native trade and industry, forced labor, lack of education, and the discouragement of enterprise.

Key Themes

  • Colonial economic policies
  • Cultural stereotypes
  • Effects of oppression
  • Historical analysis of Philippine society
  • Economic development

Historical Impact

The essay was an important contribution to the Propaganda Movement's efforts to reform Spanish colonial policy. It remains relevant in discussions about colonial mentality and economic development.

To the Young Women of Malolos

To the Young Women of Malolos (1889)

Original Title: Sa Mga Kababayang Dalaga sa Malolos

Language: Spanish (later translated to Tagalog)

Genre: Letter/Essay

Synopsis

This famous letter was written in response to a group of young women in Malolos, Bulacan who petitioned to establish a night school to study Spanish. Despite opposition from the local friar, they succeeded in their petition.

Rizal praises their courage and uses the occasion to discuss the role of women in nation-building. He emphasizes the importance of education for women, rational thinking, and the need to reject blind obedience to religious authority.

Key Themes

  • Women's education
  • Rational thinking
  • Religious hypocrisy
  • Women's role in society
  • Courage and initiative

Historical Impact

The letter became an important feminist text in Philippine history and inspired generations of Filipino women to pursue education and assert their rights. It remains a key text in Philippine women's studies.

Rizal's Legacy

National Hero Status

Rizal was officially declared the Philippines' national hero by the American colonial government in 1912. His peaceful approach to reform through education and writing made him an acceptable symbol for both American and Filipino leaders.

Rizal Day

December 30, the anniversary of Rizal's execution, is celebrated as Rizal Day, a national holiday in the Philippines.

Monuments and Memorials

  • Rizal Monument in Luneta Park, Manila - Contains his remains and is guarded 24/7
  • Rizal Shrine in Calamba, Laguna - Replica of Rizal's ancestral home
  • Rizal Park in Dapitan - Preserves his exile residence and works
  • Numerous statues, streets, and schools named after Rizal throughout the Philippines

Cultural Impact

Rizal's life and works continue to influence Philippine culture and politics:

  • His novels are required reading in Philippine schools
  • His ideals of education, nationalism, and peaceful reform remain influential
  • He is celebrated in literature, film, theater, and visual arts

International Recognition

Rizal is honored worldwide:

  • Monuments in Madrid, Spain; Wilhelmsfeld, Germany; Jinjiang, China; and other locations
  • Featured in the UNESCO Memory of the World Register for his manuscripts
  • Recognized as a significant figure in the global fight against colonialism
Rizal's Scientific Contributions

Beyond literature, Rizal made contributions to science as an ophthalmologist, linguist, ethnologist, and naturalist. He discovered several species of animals and plants in Dapitan.
